One of my less graceful decisions in 2020 was at the height of the pandemic when I purchased a fifty-count package of white paper for my impulse painting project at a whopping price of seventy-five dollars. To put this in perspective, a five-hundred pack of white paper currently sells on Amazon, Target and Bestbuy for under ten dollars. The same package of Washi paper itself is under twenty bucks. Believe it or not, I have well-earned reputation of going to great lengths to find a good deal and bypassing a nearby Whole Foods in favor of a much further Trader Joe's.  

But there I was exiting the store with a stupid grin on my face and descending down the stairwell performing a royal wave of goodbyes toward the store owner who was delighted to have made the extra large buck.


![](/images/washi-paper.jpg)

It wasn't until my partner picked up my receipt and looked at me in astonishment that I got an inkling. 

"Why did you spend $75 on paper?" he asked.

I was speechless in embarrassment. Silently, I asked myself the same question. 

Why did I not pay attention to the price? Could there have been a drug lurking in the air of the shop that impaired my thinking? Did I recall any sort of dark whispering like that of Frodo's ring luring me to open my wallet? 


The feeling in the shop was that of pure pleasure.

Minimalist, spacious and clean. Every single item seemed carefully chosen and staged for ponder and inspiration.  Every art piece and lampshade was thoughtfully placed to give pause at its beauty. 

It was on a glass shelf where I spotted a compact set of watercolors and a small paintbrush neatly packaged in a soft beige, woven box with Japanese lettering etched across the top. It was beautiful and inspiring. I could see myself carrying it in my smallest purse and then painting on a bench somewhere along the Mediterranean.  

It was my first time in a store after weeks of being in lockdown due to Covid-19 and actually all non-essential shops were still "closed." This was technically illegal but the shop owner was quietly opening its doors to those who dared to ask. Being there felt like a stroke of luck. 

By this point during the pandemic, the trend of taking on random hobbies had made its way into popular satire - perhaps out of fear of leaving the house or that maybe this was the end. Many especially those who worked in service industries could not work anymore and needed to find ways to survive.  

Forbes reported that in 2020, Etsy had more than doubled its revenues. This hobby trend and Uncle Sam checks landing in bank accounts enabled both sides of that market to thrive. 


I snatched the paint box and brought it to the shy store owner at the cashier. Realizing I had no paper, I asked if he had that as well, and he gestured toward a table. 


There it stood: a single pack of paper - a fine white light shining over it like it were the legendary Koh-I-Noor diamond itself. 

"We have hand-woven Washi." 


Never until this moment had I valued paper so highly. In this moment, all I wanted was this paper. Only handwoven paper for the princess who has not laid a finger on a paint brush since she was 10.

How can consumer psychology help me better understand the factors that drove up my valuation of the paper? 

### Emotional Context
What kind of buyer was I in that moment? What would be my buyer persona? 
I was feeling excited. I had the paint in my hand and I  Was it lack of oversight? It was an impulsive decision to buy watercolors, after all. The paper was secondary.  
 
### Retail Experience 
The store felt like more of an art gallery than a store sans the snootiness. This favorably influenced my mood and stirred up inspiration. 


### Complementary good
It was a complementary good to watercolors which I already decided to purchase. If I went home without the paper, my loss would have included the watercolors even if I had still purchased them. Prospect theory terms, I would have felt a deeper pain from not being able to use my new paints than the pain of paying considerably more to have both the watercolors and paper. 

### Scarcity 
Few stores were open during lockdown. Low supply due to covid lockdown restrictions may have pushed up my perception of actual price


### Anchoring 
Being told that the paper was "handwoven" gave the perception of high quality

### Ambiguity
Lastly, I was in a different country and that may have imparted uncertainty with regard to price level expectations.
